Question 1 (*)#

If the specific volume of a gas is 0.70 m3 /kg, what is its specific weight? {14.01 N/m3}

Given:

\[ \begin{aligned} v &= 0.700 \; \;\textrm{(m3/kg)} \end{aligned} \]

Hint

The specific weigth can be calculated as \(\gamma=\rho \cdot g\)

\(\rho\) can be calculated as \(\rho=\frac{1}{\nu}\) and we know \(g\)= 9.81 m/s2

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \rho &= \frac{ 1 }{ v } \\&= \frac{ 1 }{ 0.70 } \\&= 1.43 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \gamma &= \rho \cdot g \\&= 1.43 \cdot 9.81 \\&= 14.01 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 2 (*)[R]#

A reservoir of glycerin has a mass of 1200 kg and a volume of 0.952 m3. Find the glycerin’s weight, mass density, specific weight and specific gravity. {11.77 kN; 1261 kg/m3; 12.36 kN/m3; 1.26}

Given:

\[ \begin{aligned} m &= 1200 \; \;\textrm{(kg)} &\mathrm{Vol} &= 0.952 \; \;\textrm{(m3)} \end{aligned} \]

Hint

The weight is: \(W= m \cdot g\)

The specific weigth is: \(\gamma=\rho \cdot g\)

The mass density is: \(\rho=\frac{m}{Vol}\)

The specific gravity is: \(s= \frac {\rho_{fluid}}{\rho_{water}}\)

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} W &= m \cdot g \\&= 1200 \cdot 9.81 \\&= 11772.00 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \rho_{glycerin} &= \frac{ m }{ \mathrm{Vol} } \\&= \frac{ 1200 }{ 0.95 } \\&= 1260.50 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \gamma_{glycerin} &= \rho_{glycerin} \cdot g \\&= 1260.50 \cdot 9.81 \\&= 12365.55 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] s_{glycerin} &= \frac{ \rho_{glycerin} }{ \rho_{water} } \\&= \frac{ 1260.50 }{ 1000 } \\&= 1.26 \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 3 (*)#

The volume of a rock is found to be 0.00015 m3. If the rock’s specific gravity is 2.60, what is its weight? {3.83 N}

Given:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Vol} &= 0.00015 \; \;\textrm{(m3)} &s &= 2.60000 \; \end{aligned} \]

Hint

The weight of the rock can be calculated as \(W=\rho \cdot g \cdot Vol\)

The volume and \(g\) are known.

As we have the specific gravity, \(\rho\) can be calculated as \(\rho = \rho_{water} \cdot s\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} s_{fluid} &= 1.2 \; &m &= 2000 \; \;\textrm{(kg)} &t &= 6 \; \;\textrm{(h)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The density is: \(\rho=s \cdot \rho\)

The specific weigth is: \(\gamma=\rho \cdot g\)

The volumetric flowrate is: \(Q= \frac {Vol}{t}\)

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs

The values of \(R_{air}\), \(T\), and \(p\) are given. If we convert the temperature units to K and the pressure units to Pa, we have:

The specific weight can be calculated as \(\gamma = \frac{g \cdot p}{R_{air} \cdot T}\)

The density is \(\rho = \frac {\gamma}{g}\)

The specific volume is \(\nu =\frac{1}{\rho}\)

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \gamma &= \frac{ g \cdot p }{ R_{air} \cdot T } \\&= \frac{ 9.81 \cdot 340000.00 }{ 287 \cdot 323.15 } \\&= 35.96 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \rho &= \frac{ \gamma }{ g } \\&= \frac{ 35.96 }{ 9.81 } \\&= 3.67 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] v &= \frac{ 1 }{ \rho } \\&= \frac{ 1 }{ 3.67 } \\&= 0.27 \; \;\textrm{(m3/kg)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 11 (*)[R]#

What pressure in MPa must be applied to water to reduce its volume by 3% under standard sea level atmospheric conditions? {64.2 MPa}

Given:

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta_{Vol} &= 0.030 \; \end{aligned} \]

Hint

The compressibility of liquids is described by the following equation:

\[\Delta p =-E_v \frac{\Delta Vol}{Vol}\]

Also, From Table A.3, \(T\) under standard sea level atmospheric conditions is 288.15 K, and from Table A.1, \(E_v\) at T=288.15 K (T=15°C) is 2.14*109 Pa.

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} T &= 288.15 \; \;\textrm{(K)} \\[10pt] E_{v} &= 2140000000.00 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)} \\[10pt] \Delta_{p} &= \left( - E_{v} \right) \cdot \left( - \Delta_{Vol} \right) \\&= \left( - 2140000000.00 \right) \cdot \left( - 0.03 \right) \\&= 64200000.00 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \Delta_{p_{}} &= \frac{ \Delta_{p} }{ 1000000 } \\&= \frac{ 64200000.00 }{ 1000000 } \\&= 64.20 \; \;\textrm{(MPa)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 12 (*)#

By what fraction will the volume of a given quantity of mercury at 20°C be reduced if the pressure on it is increased by 100 MPa? {0.38%}

Given:

If we convert the pressure units to Pa, we have:

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta_{p} &= 100000000 \; \;\textrm{(MPa)} \end{aligned} \]

Hint

The reduction of volume can be calculated using the bulk modulus as follows:

\[E_v= -Vol*\frac {\Delta p}{\Delta Vol}*100\%\]
\[\frac{\Delta Vol}{Vol}=\frac{-\Delta p}{E_v} *100\%\]

Additionally, using Table A.4 the Ev is: 26.9*109 N/m2

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} E_{v} &= 26200000000.00 \; \;\textrm{(N/m2)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Vol}_{percentage} &= \left( \frac{ \left( - \Delta_{p} \right) }{ E_{v} } \right) \cdot 100 \\&= \left( \frac{ \left( - 100000000 \right) }{ 26200000000.00 } \right) \cdot 100 \\&= -0.38 \; \;\textrm{(%)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 13 (***)#

A rigid cylinder with inside diameter of 15 mm contains a column of water 500 mm long. What will the column length be if a force of 2 kN is applied to its top by a frictionless plunger? Assume a bulk modulus of 2.21 GPa. {497 mm}

Given:

The diameter and column of water in m, the force in N, and the bulk modulus in Pa:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \mathrm{dF} &= 2000 \; \;\textrm{(N)} &d &= 0.015 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &L_{1} &= 0.500 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] E_{v} &= 2210000000.000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

First, \(dp\) is calculated as \(dp=dF/A\)

Then, since the tube is rigid, the following relation applies:

\[\frac{v_2-v_1}{v_1}=\frac{L_2-L_1}{L_1}=\frac{-P_2-P_1}{E_v}\]

Solving for \(L_2\):

\[L_2 = -L_1 \cdot \frac{dP}{E_v}+ L_1\]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= \pi \cdot \left( \frac{ d }{ 2 } \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= 3.1416 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.0150 }{ 2 } \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= 0.0002 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{dp} &= \frac{ \mathrm{dF} }{ A } \\&= \frac{ 2000 }{ 0.0002 } \\&= 11317684.8421 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] L_{2} &= \left( - L_{1} \right) \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{dp} }{ E_{v} } + L_{1} \\&= \left( - 0.5000 \right) \cdot \frac{ 11317684.8421 }{ 2210000000.0000 } + 0.5000 \\&= 0.4974 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] L_{2_{}} &= 497.4394 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Viscosity#

Question 4 (*)#

A square block has a contact area of 0.02 m2 with a table that is lubricated with a 0.1 mm thick layer of oil with viscosity 7 mPa.s. What force is required to move the block at a constant velocity of 1 m/s? {1.4 N}

Given:

If we convert the depth of the layer to m, and viscosity to Pa/s, we have the following given parameters:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= 0.020 \; \;\textrm{(m2)} &y &= 0.100 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} &\mu_{oil} &= 0.007 \; \;\textrm{(Pa/s)} \\[10pt] \Delta_{V} &= 1 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

At a constant velocity, aceleration is zero, therefore:

\[\begin{split} \sum F=0\\ \end{split}\]

Force can be calculated as

\[\begin{split} F-\tau A=0\\ \end{split}\]

Where

\[ \tau = \mu \frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y} \]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \Delta_{y} &= \frac{ y }{ 1000 } \\&= \frac{ 0.1000 }{ 1000 } \\&= 0.0001 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau &= \mu_{oil} \cdot \left( \frac{ \Delta_{V} }{ \Delta_{y} } \right) \\&= 0.0070 \cdot \left( \frac{ 1 }{ 0.0001 } \right) \\&= 70.0000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= \tau \cdot A \\&= 70.0000 \cdot 0.0200 \\&= 1.4000 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 5 (**)[R]#

A space 16 mm wide between two large plane surfaces is filled with oil with a viscosity μ = 0.18 N.s/m2. What force F will be required to drag a very thin plate of 0.4 m2 area between the surfaces at a speed of 0.25 m/s if the plate is positioned 5 mm from the bottom surface? {5.24 N}

Given:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \mu &= 0.180 \; \;\textrm{(N.s/m2)} &V &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)} &y_{plate} &= 0.005 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] y_{oil} &= 0.016 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &A &= 0.400 \; \;\textrm{(m2)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

To calculate the force required to drag the plate, the Newton’s equation of viscosity can be used:

\[\begin{split} \tau= \mu\frac{dV}{dy}\\ \end{split}\]
\[ F=\tau A \]

The total force is the sum between the force of the oil and the force of the plate

\[\begin{split} F_{oil}= \mu\frac{dV}{(y_{oil}-y_{plate})} A\\ \end{split}\]
\[ F_{plate}= \mu\frac{dV}{dy} A \]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \tau_{oil} &= \mu \cdot \left( \frac{ V }{ y_{oil} - y_{plate} } \right) \\&= 0.180 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.250 }{ 0.016 - 0.005 } \right) \\&= 4.091 \; \;\textrm{(N/m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau_{plate} &= \mu \cdot \left( \frac{ V }{ y_{plate} } \right) \\&= 0.180 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.250 }{ 0.005 } \right) \\&= 9.000 \; \;\textrm{(N/m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F_{oil} &= \tau_{oil} \cdot A \\&= 4.091 \cdot 0.400 \\&= 1.636 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F_{plate} &= \tau_{plate} \cdot A \\&= 9.000 \cdot 0.400 \\&= 3.600 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= F_{oil} + F_{plate} \\&= 1.636 + 3.600 \\&= 5.236 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

If a constant force of 10 N is applied the plate, at what speed will it be dragged out from between the surfaces? {0.278 m/s}

Given:

If we convert the space between the surfaces to m is:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= 0.500 \; \;\textrm{(m2)} &L &= 0.010 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\mu &= 0.180 \; \;\textrm{(N.s/m2)} \\[10pt] F &= 10 \; \;\textrm{(N)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

Force can be calculated as:

\[\begin{split} F=A\mu \frac {\Delta v}{\Delta y}\\ \end{split}\]

Where the total Area is \(1m^2\) because the area of one plate is \(A= 0.5 m^2\).

Solving for \(\Delta v\):

\[ \Delta V =F\frac {\Delta y}{\mu A_{total}} \]

and \(\Delta y\) is half of the distance between the plane surfaces

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} L &= \frac{ L }{ 2 } \\&= \frac{ 0.005 }{ 2 } \\&= 0.005 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] A_{total} &= A \cdot 2 \\&= 0.500 \cdot 2 \\&= 1.000 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] V &= F \cdot \frac{ L }{ \mu \cdot A_{total} } \\&= 10 \cdot \frac{ 0.005 }{ 0.180 \cdot 1.000 } \\&= 0.278 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 7 (**)[R]#

A square block weighing 1.1 kN and with side lengths of 250 mm slides down a 20 ° incline on a film of oil 6.0 micrometre thick. Assuming a linear velocity profile in the oil, what is the terminal velocity of the block? The viscosity of the oil is 7 mPa.s. {5.16 m/s}

Given:

If we convert the weight to N, the length to m, and the thick to m:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} W &= 1100.000000 \; \;\textrm{(N)} &\theta &= 20 \; \;\textrm{(°)} &\mathrm{dy} &= 0.000006 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \mu &= 0.007000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa.s)} &L &= 0.250000 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

At terminal velocity, the block is moving at a constant speed, meaning the net force acting on it is zero. By considering the forces along each axis, the following equation can be derived:

\[\begin{split} \sum F_x=0\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_\mu-Wsin\theta=0\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_\mu=Wsin\theta\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_\mu=\tau A\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_\mu=\mu \frac{dV}{dy} A\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} \mu \frac{dV}{dy} A=Wsin\theta\\ \end{split}\]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= \left( L \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= \left( 0.25 \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= 0.06 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{dV} &= \frac{ W \cdot \sin \left( \operatorname{radians} \theta \right) \cdot \mathrm{dy} }{ A \cdot \mu } \\&= \frac{ 1100.00 \cdot \sin \left( \operatorname{radians} 20 \right) \cdot 0.00 }{ 0.06 \cdot 0.01 } \\&= 5.16 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 8 (***)#

A hydraulic lift consist of a 280 mm diameter ram which slides in a 280.18 mm cylinder, the annular space being filled with oil having a kinematic viscosity of 0.00042 m2/s and a specific gravity of 0.86. If the rate of travel of the ram is 0.22 m/s, find the frictional resistance when 2 m of the ram is engaged in the cylinder. {1553 N}

Given:

If we convert the diameters of the hydraulic lift and the cilinder to m, we have the following parameters:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d_{lift} &= 0.2800 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &d_{cyl} &= 0.2802 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\nu &= 0.0004 \; \;\textrm{(m2/s)} \\[10pt] s &= 0.8600 \; &\Delta_{V} &= 0.2200 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)} &h &= 2 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

The frition resistance can be calculated as \(F=\tau A\)

Where \(A=h \cdot \pi \cdot d_{lift}\) and \(\tau = \mu \frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y}\)

The dynamic viscosity can be calculated as \(\mu = \nu \cdot \rho\), where \(\rho = s \cdot \rho_{water}\)

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \rho &= s \cdot \rho_{water} \\&= 0.860 \cdot 1000 \\&= 860.000 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mu &= \nu \cdot \rho \\&= 0.000 \cdot 860.000 \\&= 0.361 \; \;\textrm{(Pa.s)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \Delta_{y} &= \frac{ d_{cyl} - d_{lift} }{ 2 } \\&= \frac{ 0.280 - 0.280 }{ 2 } \\&= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau &= \mu \cdot \frac{ \Delta_{V} }{ \Delta_{y} } \\&= 0.361 \cdot \frac{ 0.220 }{ 0.000 } \\&= 882.933 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] A &= h \cdot \pi \cdot d_{lift} \\&= 2 \cdot 3.142 \cdot 0.280 \\&= 1.759 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= \tau \cdot A \\&= 882.933 \cdot 1.759 \\&= 1553.337 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 9 (***)#

A piston with diameter of 127 mm, height 152 mm and weight 93.4 N slides free in a lubricated vertical pipe. The clearance between the piston and the pipe is 0.0254 mm. If the piston decelerates at 0.64 m/s2 when its speed is 6.4 m/s, what is the viscosity of the oil? { 0.0065 N.s/m2 }

Given:

If we convert the diameter, the height, and the clearance between the piston and the pipe to meters, we have the following parameters:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d &= 0.127 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &h &= 0.152 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &W &= 93.400 \; \;\textrm{(N)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{dy} &= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\mathrm{dV} &= 0.640 \; \;\textrm{(m/s2)} &a &= 0.640 \; \;\textrm{(m/s2)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

In this case, since there is a deceleration, the sum of forces along the axis must be set equal to mass times acceleration. Including all the forces in the equation, we have the following

\[\begin{split} \sum F=ma\\ F_\tau - W=ma\\ \end{split}\]

and \(F_\tau\) is:

\[\begin{split} F_\tau=\mu \frac{dV}{dy} A\\ \mu \frac{dV}{dy} A - W =ma \end{split}\]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= 2 \cdot \pi \cdot \left( \frac{ d }{ 2 } \right) \cdot h \\&= 2 \cdot 3.142 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.127 }{ 2 } \right) \cdot 0.152 \\&= 0.061 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] m &= \frac{ W }{ g } \\&= \frac{ 93.400 }{ 9.810 } \\&= 9.521 \; \;\textrm{(kg)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mu &= \left( \frac{ m \cdot a + W }{ A \cdot \mathrm{dV} } \right) \cdot \mathrm{dy} \\&= \left( \frac{ 9.521 \cdot 0.640 + 93.400 }{ 0.061 \cdot 0.640 } \right) \cdot 0.000 \\&= 0.065 \; \;\textrm{(N.s/m2)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 10 (***)#

A journal bearing consists of an 80.0 mm diameter shaft in an 80.4 mm sleeve 120 mm long. The clearance space is filled with oil with a viscosity of 0.11 N.s/m2. Calculate the torque required to keep the shaft turning at 150 rpm. {0.417 N.m}

Given:

The given parameters are the following: (Note that the diameters and length were converted to meters).

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d_{journal} &= 0.080 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &d_{sleeve} &= 0.080 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\mathrm{long}_{sleeve} &= 0.120 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \mu_{filled} &= 0.110 \; \;\textrm{(Ns/m2)} &N_{rpm} &= 150 \; \;\textrm{(rpm)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

The torque can be calculated as \(Torque = F \cdot r_{journal}\)

Where

\[ \tau= \mu\frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y} \]

In the equation the velocity needs to be in m/s. For that reason, the units have to be converted.

\[ V= wr \]
\[ w= \frac {2 \pi N}{60} \]
\[ V=\frac{r_{journal}\cdot2\cdot\pi\cdot N_{rpm}}{60} \]

The torque is calculated by multiplying the force with the distance

\[ T= F \cdot r_{journal} \]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} r_{journal} &= \frac{ d_{journal} }{ 2 } \\&= \frac{ 0.080 }{ 2 } \\&= 0.040 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] V &= \frac{ r_{journal} \cdot 2 \cdot \pi \cdot N_{rpm} }{ 60 } \\&= \frac{ 0.040 \cdot 2 \cdot 3.142 \cdot 150 }{ 60 } \\&= 0.628 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \Delta_{y} &= \frac{ d_{sleeve} - d_{journal} }{ 2 } \\&= \frac{ 0.080 - 0.080 }{ 2 } \\&= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau &= \mu_{filled} \cdot \frac{ V }{ \Delta_{y} } \\&= 0.110 \cdot \frac{ 0.628 }{ 0.000 } \\&= 345.575 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= \tau \cdot \pi \cdot d_{journal} \cdot \mathrm{long}_{sleeve} \\&= 345.575 \cdot 3.142 \cdot 0.080 \cdot 0.120 \\&= 10.422 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] T &= F \cdot r_{journal} \\&= 10.422 \cdot 0.040 \\&= 0.417 \; \;\textrm{(N.m)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 12 (*)[R]#

A glass tube with diameter 1.5 mm is dipped vertically in water. Calculate from basic principles how high the water will rise in the tube due to capillary action. Take the coefficient of surface tension to be 0.0730 N/m and the angle of contact as 10°. {19.5 mm}

Given:

If we convert the diameter to meters, we have the following given parameters:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d_{tube} &= 0.002 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\sigma &= 0.073 \; \;\textrm{(N/m)} &\theta &= 10 \; \;\textrm{(°)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

By equating the lifting force created by surface tension to the gravity force, we obtain the following:

\[\begin{split} \sum F =0 \\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_{\sigma}-W=0\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_{\sigma}=\sigma cos(\theta) \pi d\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} W = \frac {\pi d^2 h\rho g}{4}\\ \end{split}\]

Replacing the \(F_{\sigma}\) and \(W\)

\[ \sigma cos(\theta) \pi d-\frac {\pi d^2 h\rho g}{4}=0 \]

Calculating \(F_{sigma}\) and solving for \(h\):

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \theta_{rad} &= \operatorname{radians} \left( \theta \right) \\&= \operatorname{radians} \left( 10 \right) \\&= 0.1745 \; \;\textrm{(rad)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F_{\sigma} &= \sigma \cdot \pi \cdot d_{tube} \cdot \cos \left( \theta_{rad} \right) \\&= 0.0730 \cdot 3.1416 \cdot 0.0015 \cdot \cos \left( 0.1745 \right) \\&= 0.0003 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] h &= F_{\sigma} \cdot \frac{ 4 }{ \left( d_{tube} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot \rho_{water} \cdot g \cdot \pi } \\&= 0.0003 \cdot \frac{ 4 }{ \left( 0.0015 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 1000 \cdot 9.8100 \cdot 3.1416 } \\&= 0.0195 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] h_{mm} &= h_{} \\&= h \cdot 1000 \\&= 19.5422 \\&= 0.0195 \cdot 1000 \\&= 19.5422 \; \;\textrm{(mm)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 13 (*)#

A glass tube with diameter 1 mm is dipped vertically in water. Calculate from basic principles how high the water will rise in the tube due to capillary action. Take the coefficient of surface tension to be 0.0730 N/m and the angle of contact as 20°. {28 mm}

Given:

If we convert the diameter to meters, we have the following parameters:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d &= 0.001 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\sigma &= 0.073 \; \;\textrm{(N/m)} &\theta &= 20 \; \;\textrm{(°)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hint

To calculate the height of the water, it is necessary to perform a force balance along that axis, and include all the forces.

\[\begin{split} \sum F_y=0\\ \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} F_\sigma - W= 0\\ \end{split}\]
\[ \sigma cos(\theta) \cdot \pi \cdot d - \frac{\pi \cdot d^2}{4} \cdot h \cdot \rho \cdot g =0 \]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} h &= \frac{ 4 \cdot \sigma \cdot \cos \left( \operatorname{radians} \theta \right) }{ d \cdot \rho_{water} \cdot g } \\&= \frac{ 4 \cdot 0.073 \cdot \cos \left( \operatorname{radians} 20 \right) }{ 0.001 \cdot 1000 \cdot 9.810 } \\&= 0.028 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] h_{mm} &= h \cdot 1000 \\&= 0.028 \cdot 1000 \\&= 27.970 \; \;\textrm{(mm)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]
